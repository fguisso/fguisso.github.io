<SectionDivider label="01 // ORIGINS · THE NETWORK THAT TALKED" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px">
		<h2>When the <span class="ph-accent-blue">network</span> still spoke out loud</h2>
		<p>
			Until the 1990s, the global phone system was dominated by <strong>AT&T</strong> and its
			national counterparts. It was the largest engineering project of the 20th century before
			the internet: a network of electromechanical switches (later electronic, with the ESS,
			<em>Electronic Switching Systems</em>) covering entire continents over copper wire and
			relay-based central offices.
		</p>
		<p>
			The defining feature, and the fatal flaw, was <strong class="ph-accent-orange">in-band
			signaling</strong>: the commands that controlled a call (set up, tear down, route) were
			audio tones transmitted <em>on the same frequency band as the human voice</em>. The switch
			had no separate control channel; it listened to the same wire that carried your conversation.
		</p>
		<p>
			An idle long-distance trunk continuously emitted a <strong>2600 Hz</strong> tone. When
			someone placed a call, the tone stopped, signaling "line in use". When the call ended, the
			tone returned, signaling "trunk free, next customer please". The consequence was simple and
			devastating: <em>if you could generate 2600 Hz into the receiver, the switch believed your
			call had ended, without you hanging up.</em>
		</p>
		<p>
			AT&T published all the technical details in the <strong>Bell System Technical
			Journal</strong> (November 1960, <em>"Signaling Systems for Control of Telephone
			Switching"</em>). The assumption was naive but honest: no ordinary customer would own a
			calibrated tone generator, and Bell engineers were trustworthy men. It took one generation
			to turn that trust into a billion-dollar hole.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="02 // JOYBUBBLES · THE PERFECT EAR" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px">
		<h2><span class="ph-accent-orange">Joybubbles</span>, the boy who talked to the network</h2>
		<p>
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joybubbles" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Josef
			Carl Engressia Jr.</strong></a>, born May 25, 1949 in Richmond, Virginia, was the first
			phreaker recorded by history. He was born blind and had <strong>absolute pitch</strong>.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-howbox">
			<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ WHAT IS ABSOLUTE PITCH?</div>
			<div>
				A rare ability (estimated at 1 in 10,000 people) to identify and reproduce any musical
				note without a reference. Someone with absolute pitch hears a sound and knows
				immediately whether it's A-440Hz, E-330Hz or G#-415Hz, the same way most people
				recognize colors. Joybubbles' gift was even rarer: he could <em>whistle</em> any
				specific frequency on demand, with just a few hertz of variation. For a phone system that
				controlled calls with calibrated tones, that was handing him the keys to the vault.
			</div>
		</div>
		<p>
			By age 4 he already spent hours on the phone. He soon grew fascinated by the thousands
			of distinct tones the network produced, and started listening to them for hours every day.
			2600 Hz wasn't a common tone on regular calls; he only began to hear it after spending
			long stretches with the phone off the hook, listening to the silence of the idle
			long-distance trunk. When he recognized that specific tone, he started whistling it back:
			he didn't yet know what it did, but he knew it was a valid system signal. He had found
			2600 Hz at age 7, in 1957, <strong>thirteen years</strong> before Captain Crunch and the
			cereal whistle. Even before the whistle, still a kid, Joe found he could replicate
			rotary-dial pulses with the hookswitch and bypass the lock his babysitter clamped on the
			phone: the first practical record of tap dialing.
		</p>
		<p>
			In 1968, a student at the <strong>University of South Florida</strong>, Joe was the subject
			of a campus newspaper article that nicknamed him <em>"The Whistler"</em>. AT&T sued the
			university. In 1971, Ron Rosenbaum interviewed him for the Esquire piece
			<a href="https://www.slate.com/articles/technology/the_spectator/2011/10/the_article_that_inspired_steve_jobs_secrets_of_the_little_blue_.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>"Secrets of the Little Blue Box"</em></a>,
			the same text that would inspire
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Wozniak</a>
			to build the first Blue Box.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-trivia">
			<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ HE NEVER WANTED THE MONEY</div>
			Joe never used his skills for fraud: he wanted to <em>understand the network</em>. In 1975,
			after years of friction with the FBI, he was hired by <strong>Mountain Bell</strong> in
			Denver as a network troubleshooter, a role he held for seven years until moving to
			Minneapolis in 1982. In May 1988 he "reverted to childhood" as a way to process sexual
			abuse he had suffered as a child, declaring himself permanently 5 years old. In 1991, he
			formalized the change in court and legally adopted the name
			<strong class="ph-accent-orange">"Joybubbles"</strong>. He died on August 8, 2007 in
			Minneapolis of congestive heart failure.
			<div style="margin-top:14px;font-size:13px;opacity:0.85">
				▸ Worth listening to NPR's obituary:
				<a href="https://www.npr.org/transcripts/13764518" target="_blank" rel="noopener">"Joe
				Engressia, Expert 'Phone Phreak,' Dies"</a> (2007).
			</div>
		</div>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="03 // THE TAP · HOOKSWITCH DIALING" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-grid-2" style="margin-bottom:40px">
		<div>
			<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">tap</span>: hookswitch dialing</h2>
			<p>
				The oldest phreaking technique used no special tones or whistles, just a well-trained
				finger. Called <strong>tapping</strong> or <em>switch-hook dialing</em>, it turned
				the phone's hookswitch into an improvised dial. Joybubbles learned the trick as a
				kid, before he ever discovered the magic whistle.
			</p>
			<p>
				To understand why the trick works, it helps to see what it was imitating: the rotary
				dial telephone (alongside). Each digit spun the disk to a finger stop; on its way
				back, an internal cam rotated and its teeth struck a copper pawl, generating the
				electrical pulses the switch counted.
			</p>
		</div>
		<RotaryDial locale="en" />
	</div>

	<div class="ph-grid-2">
		<div>
			<h3>How tapping beats the dial</h3>
			<p>
				Dialing "5" meant sending 5 pulses at a cadence of <strong>10 pulses per second</strong>
				(60ms open / 40ms closed). Phreakers found the shortcut: <em>tapping the hookswitch
				repeatedly</em> opens and closes the same circuit. Same logic as the dial, no dial
				needed. Phones with a locked rotary? Bypass via hookswitch. Hotel payphones with no
				dial? Tap the hook. It was the most democratic trick in phreaking, any finger learned
				it in half an hour.
			</p>
			<div class="ph-howbox">
				<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ HOW IT WORKED</div>
				<div>
					1. Pick up the phone (line closed → dial tone)<br/>
					2. To dial "3", tap the hookswitch 3 times (~60ms each)<br/>
					3. Wait ~700ms, the switch registers "3"<br/>
					4. Repeat for the other digits. "0" is 10 taps.
				</div>
			</div>
			<div class="ph-trivia">
				<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ POP CULTURE</div>
				The classic scene of a prisoner who needs to make a forbidden call, hangs up the
				prison payphone and <em>taps the hookswitch</em> to dial a number off the approved
				list, is a direct reference to this technique. It shows up in
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hackers_(film)" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Hackers (1995)</strong></a>,
				in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WarGames" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>WarGames (1983)</em></a>,
				and in a dozen prison thrillers from the 80s and 90s. It wasn't a screenwriter's
				invention: it was the only thing that actually worked on a phone with a locked dial.
			</div>
		</div>
		<div>
			<TapDialer locale="en" />
			<figure class="ph-figure" style="margin-top:16px">
				<Image src={lockedPhone} alt="Rotary phone with a physical padlock through the dial" widths={[400, 800]} sizes="(max-width: 768px) 90vw, 420px" />

			</figure>
		</div>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="04 // THE 2600 Hz WHISTLE" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-grid-2">
		<div>
			<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">2600 Hz</span> whistle</h2>
			<p>
				Joybubbles was the first to notice, but not the only one. In 1968, engineer
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Draper" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong class="ph-accent-orange">John Draper</strong></a>
				met a blind sailor who gave him the tip: the plastic toy whistle that came as a prize
				in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cap%27n_Crunch" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Cap'n Crunch</a>
				cereal played exactly 2600 Hz. Blow it into the mouthpiece and the long-distance trunk
				surrendered. The world got a <em>Captain Crunch</em>; AT&T lost control.
			</p>
			<WhistleViewer locale="en" />
			<p>
				When the whistle hit the receiver, the switch believed the call had ended, but the line
				stayed open. You were still connected to a live long-distance trunk, only now <em>as if
				you were the switch yourself</em>, free to issue direct commands.
			</p>
			<div class="ph-howbox">
				<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ HOW IT WORKED</div>
				<div>
					1. Dial a 0800 number (free, but uses a long-distance trunk)<br/>
					2. After connection, blow 2600 Hz into the mouthpiece<br/>
					3. The remote office disconnects the destination but keeps the trunk<br/>
					4. You now have direct access to the trunk network
				</div>
			</div>
		</div>
		<FrequencySimulator locale="en" />
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="05 // BLUE BOX & CCITT5" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="margin-bottom:40px;max-width:800px">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-blue">Blue Box</span> and MF tones</h2>
		<p>
			Once the 2600 Hz whistle "opened" the trunk, you had to <em>tell the network</em> where to
			dial. AT&T used a signaling system called <strong class="ph-accent-blue">CCITT5 / Multi-Frequency (MF)</strong>:
			each digit is represented by <strong>two simultaneous tones</strong>, picked from a matrix of
			6 frequencies (700, 900, 1100, 1300, 1500, 1700 Hz).
		</p>
		<p>
			The <strong class="ph-accent-blue">Blue Box</strong>, popularized by
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Wozniak</a>
			and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Jobs</a>
			before they founded Apple, was a device that generated those tone pairs. Ron Rosenbaum's
			article <a href="https://www.slate.com/articles/technology/the_spectator/2011/10/the_article_that_inspired_steve_jobs_secrets_of_the_little_blue_.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>"Secrets of the Little Blue Box"</em></a>,
			published in Esquire in 1971, exploded the popularity of the hobby.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-howbox">
			<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ BEFORE YOU PLAY: KP AND ST</div>
			<div>
				On top of the 10 digits, the MF protocol carries two <em>control</em> tones that tell
				the remote switch when an address starts and ends. Without them, the digit sequence is
				just noise.<br/><br/>
				• <strong class="ph-accent-blue">KP</strong> (<em>Key Pulse</em>, tones
				<span class="ph-mono">1100 + 1700 Hz</span>) opens the packet: it says
				"<em>heads up, digits are coming</em>". Always the first tone sent.<br/>
				• <strong class="ph-accent-blue">ST</strong> (<em>Start</em>, tones
				<span class="ph-mono">1500 + 1700 Hz</span>) closes the packet: "<em>I'm done, you
				can route now</em>". Always the last.<br/><br/>
				The full sequence for a call is <span class="ph-mono">KP · digits · ST</span>. In the
				simulator below, start with KP, dial the destination digits, and finish with ST.
			</div>
		</div>
	</div>
	<BlueBox locale="en" />
</section>

<SectionDivider label="07 // MODERN ATTACKS · 2018–2025" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px;margin-bottom:16px">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">ghosts</span> changed instruments</h2>
		<p>
			Digital telephony killed analog phreaking, but traded one set of fragile protocols for
			another. SS7, Diameter, GTP, SIGTRAN, IMS: stacks designed in the 1970s–1990s, when the
			network was a closed club of trusted carriers. Today, any actor with access to a
			<em>roaming hub</em> or a US$ 500 SDR enters as an authorized peer.
		</p>
	</div>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">05.1</span> SS7 / SIGTRAN</h3>
	<p style="max-width:900px">
		The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_System_No._7" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SS7 protocol</a>
		(1975) and its IP version, SIGTRAN, are the baseline signaling for 2G/3G worldwide.
		<strong>No authentication</strong>, no encryption. Anyone inside the network can query a
		subscriber's location, redirect SMS, or intercept calls. In 2019, fraudsters
		<a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/criminals-hackers-ss7-uk-banks-metro-bank/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">exploited SS7 in the UK</a>
		to intercept 2FA codes and drain <strong>Metro Bank</strong> accounts. In Brazil, similar
		fraud was already documented since 2016. The
		<a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/signalling-security-in-telecom-ss7-diameter-5g" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2018 ENISA report</a>
		formally acknowledges the technical debt: 2G/3G rely on SS7 with no "modern security
		considerations", and 4G (Diameter) inherited the problem.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">05.2</span> Femtocells and rogue base stations</h3>
	<p style="max-width:900px">
		In 2013, <strong>Doug DePerry</strong> and <strong>Tom Ritter</strong> showed at
		<a href="https://www.defcon.org/images/defcon-21/dc-21-presentations/DePerry-Ritter/DEFCON-21-DePerry-Ritter-Femtocell-Updated.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DEF CON 21</a>
		how to modify a Verizon <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femtocell" target="_blank" rel="noopener">femtocell</a>
		(US$ 250) plus a Raspberry Pi (US$ 50) to listen to every call and SMS from nearby phones.
		The whole rig fit in a backpack. Twelve years later, the same technique came back at scale:
		commercial gear like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stingray_phone_tracker" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>StingRay</strong></a>
		(Harris Corp.) and homemade variants with <strong>USRP +
		<a href="https://openbts.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OpenBTS</a> /
		<a href="https://osmocom.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Osmocom</a></strong> are used
		by states, gangs and researchers. Phones connect to the strongest signal, and when the
		strongest signal is hostile, hostile wins.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">05.3</span> SIM Swap and number portability</h3>
	<p style="max-width:900px">
		A telco support agent talked into transferring a victim's line to an attacker-controlled SIM.
		Within seconds, every 2FA SMS, bank, email, exchange, goes to the wrong phone.
		<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIM_swap_scam" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>SIM swap</strong></a>
		doesn't exploit a protocol: it exploits people. But the metrics climb with SMS-based 2FA. The
		FBI and Brazil's ANATEL issued public warnings in the early 2020s.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">05.4</span> VoLTE / IMS / SMS Blasting</h3>
	<p style="max-width:900px">
		Voice over LTE (VoLTE) brought IMS, a full SIP stack inside the carrier. Researchers
		demonstrated downgrade and interception attacks on VoLTE calls in 2021. In parallel,
		<strong>SIM Boxes</strong>, racks with hundreds of SIM cards running in parallel, fire tens
		of thousands of SMS per hour for financial fraud. Same idea as the Blue Box, industrialized
		(see Section 08).
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">05.5</span> PBX and VoIP fraud</h3>
	<p style="max-width:900px">
		Misconfigured corporate PBXs keep dialing premium-rate international numbers overnight.
		Hacked ATA adapters, weak SIP credentials, exposed VoIP gateways, each one is a modern
		<em>blue box</em>, generating five-figure invoices for the victim and dividends for the
		attacker.
	</p>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="09 // ABANDONED FORUMS · 2000–2015" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-blue">underground</span> goes digital</h2>
		<p>
			Between 2000 and 2015, what was left of analog phreaking merged with the nascent
			<em>"digital phreaking"</em> on forums like <span class="ph-mono" style="color:var(--ph-yellow)">alt.phone.phreaking</span>,
			private Telnet lists, abandoned FTPs and researcher blogs. Primary sources from that era
			are fragile: many archives only survive today on the
			<a href="https://archive.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Internet Archive</a> or community
			mirrors like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hack_Canada" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Hack Canada</strong></a> and
			<a href="https://2600.com/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>2600 Magazine</strong></a>.
		</p>
		<p>
			Projects like <a href="http://www.projectmf.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><span class="ph-mono" style="color:var(--ph-yellow)">Project MF</span></a>
			(Arduino-based Blue Box reconstruction with an Asterisk server simulating a 1970s Class 4
			toll switch), <a href="https://openbts.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>OpenBTS</strong></a>
			(software GSM base station) and
			<a href="https://osmocom.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Osmocom</strong></a>
			show how knowledge migrated from "audio hacking" to protocol reverse engineering. Talks at
			<a href="https://defcon.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DEF CON</a>, ToorCon, CCC
			and HOPE documented the transition on video. Most of the academic literature only arrives
			after 2015, before that, the data lived in forums that closed, one by one.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="10 // RESISTANCE UNDER AUTHORITARIAN REGIMES" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px;margin-bottom:24px">
		<h2>When the state <span class="ph-accent-orange">switches off</span> the network</h2>
		<p>
			When governments cut mobile or fixed internet to silence protests, communities improvise.
			The result is an unlikely catalog: amateur radio, V.92 modems crossing a border, mesh
			networks, LoRaWAN, satellite. The official network falls; others emerge.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-cards-grid">
		<div class="ph-case-card is-orange">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>2020</span><span>BELARUS</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Modems crossing borders</div>
			<p>
				During the <a href="https://www.hrw.org/news/2020/08/28/belarus-internet-disruptions-online-censorship" target="_blank" rel="noopener">protests against Lukashenko</a>,
				the government cut internet and mobile for 61 hours. Activists and journalists fell
				back on V.92 modems over international landlines, dialing into foreign ISPs as
				emergency gateways. <strong>56 kbps is infinitely better than 0 kbps.</strong>
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>JAN/2022</span><span>KAZAKHSTAN</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Blackout in Zhanaozen</div>
			<p>
				Total mobile internet blackout during the protests. DSL landlines and V.92 dial-up
				were the only route abroad. International NGOs documented the blackout, but local
				infrastructure couldn't carry traffic at scale.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-orange">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>AUG/2023</span><span>NAGORNO-KARABAKH</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Lachin fiber cut</div>
			<p>
				<a href="https://armenpress.am/eng/news/1117619/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">On 17/08/2023</a>, Azerbaijani forces cut the fiber cable connecting Artsakh, leaving the
				region offline. Residual traffic survived over <strong>HF and amateur radio</strong>,
				under continuous jamming. Russian peacekeeper cameras tried to restore links via radio.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-blue">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>2014–TODAY</span><span>OCCUPIED UKRAINE</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Ghost carriers in Donetsk and Luhansk</div>
			<p>
				Separatist zones built their own mobile carriers with hardware seized from
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifecell_(Ukraine)" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Lifecell</strong></a>
				and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kyivstar" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Kyivstar</strong></a>:
				Phoenix (Feniks) in Donetsk; Lugacom (later MCS) in Luhansk. They kept the Ukrainian
				MCC (255) until 2022, when they began migrating to Russian +7 prefixes.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-blue">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>2001–2019</span><span>CUBA</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNET_(Cuba)" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SNET</a>, the Street Network
			</div>
			<p>
				Community Wi-Fi mesh with up to <strong>100,000 nodes</strong> in Havana. No public
				internet dependency. It had games, forums, local voice, file repositories. Absorbed by
				the state in 2019, but it proved an entire city can run its own parallel network.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>2021–TODAY</span><span>MYANMAR</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Post-coup: ham radio and Starlink</div>
			<p>
				After the Feb/2021 military coup, the junta shut down mobile internet repeatedly.
				Resistance leaned on amateur radio, mesh networks with <strong>LoRaWAN</strong>, and
				smuggled Starlink terminals (some confiscated by the military).
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-blue">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>2019–2022</span><span>HONG KONG / IRAN</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">Mesh over Bluetooth</div>
			<p>
				Apps like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridgefy" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Bridgefy</strong></a>
				and <a href="https://briarproject.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Briar</strong></a>
				became communication channels during protests. Messages hopped phone-to-phone over
				Bluetooth and Wi-Fi Direct, never touching a carrier. Vulnerabilities surfaced later,
				but the paradigm stuck.
			</p>
		</div>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="11 // BRAZIL · 2024–2025" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div style="max-width:900px;margin-bottom:24px">
		<h2>Antennas in <span class="ph-accent-orange">apartments</span> and ghost cars</h2>
		<p>
			Brazil entered the era of <em>gang phreaking</em>. Across 2024 and 2025, São Paulo state
			police dismantled three sophisticated operations using clandestine telecom hardware for
			large-scale banking fraud. The MO is industrial: knock victims off the legit signal, force
			them onto a rogue BTS controlled by the gang, blast SMS with phishing links.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-cards-grid">
		<div class="ph-case-card is-orange">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>23/JUL/2024</span><span>SP, AV. FARIA LIMA</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">
				<a href="https://www.cnnbrasil.com.br/nacional/carro-do-golpe-veja-equipamentos-encontrados-em-veiculo-para-interceptar-sinal-de-celulares/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Antenna car along Faria Lima</a>
			</div>
			<p>
				The driver of a rented car caught red-handed after patrol. He was circling 8–12 hours a
				day along Av. Paulista and Av. Faria Lima with transmitters that
				<strong>knocked nearby phones off 4G</strong>. When the signal dropped, the rig fired
				SMS with a fake banking link. He confessed to police he was paid
				<strong>R$ 1,000/week</strong> just to drive.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-orange">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>23/JAN/2025</span><span>SP, MORUMBI</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">
				<a href="https://sbtnews.sbt.com.br/noticia/policia/esquema-de-sms-falso-e-descoberto-em-predio-na-zona-oeste-de-sao-paulo" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Antenna on a balcony, 19th floor</a>
			</div>
			<p>
				Apartment on the <strong>19th floor</strong> of a Morumbi (West Zone) building, with an
				antenna mounted on the balcony aimed at Marginal Pinheiros highway. The 2G rig
				<strong>jammed 3G/4G/5G</strong> in a 2 km radius. A single antenna can fire over
				<strong>100,000 messages per day</strong>. ANATEL and Polícia Civil dismantled the
				operation after a 6-month investigation.
			</p>
		</div>
		<div class="ph-case-card is-orange">
			<div class="ph-case-meta"><span>10/SEP/2025</span><span>SP, ITAQUERA</span></div>
			<div class="ph-case-title">
				<a href="https://www.metropoles.com/sao-paulo/policia-descobre-central-movel-do-golpe-do-sms-e-prende-motorista" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Mobile femtocell, antenna car v2</a>
			</div>
			<p>
				Another police operation, another antenna car. A 35-year-old firing
				<strong>~40,000 SMS/hour</strong> from inside the vehicle in the East Zone, also
				targeting Av. Paulista and Pinheiros. Radio transmitters and directional antennas
				seized. Charged with criminal association and unauthorized access to computing devices.
			</p>
		</div>
	</div>

	<p style="max-width:900px;margin-top:24px">
		The common denominator: carrier-grade hardware (femtocells originally sold as residential
		repeaters), patched firmware, directional antennas, and software derived from OpenBTS / YateBTS.
		The technical ecosystem researchers demoed in 2013 reached retail crime ten years later.
	</p>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="14 // LEGACY AND FINAL THOUGHTS" />

<section class="ph-outro">
	<p>
		In the 1980s, AT&T began migrating to the <strong>CCS7
		(Common Channel Signaling)</strong> system, which separates signaling from the audio channel. From
		that point on, no audible tone could control the network anymore, analog phreaking was dead.
	</p>
	<p>
		But its legacy is immense. <strong>Wozniak</strong> and <strong>Jobs</strong> sold blue boxes in
		UC Berkeley dorms before founding Apple. <strong>Joybubbles</strong> became folklore. The magazine
		<em>2600: The Hacker Quarterly</em>, founded in 1984, takes its name from the magical tone. And the
		ethos, radical curiosity, reverse engineering closed systems, became the DNA of hacker culture.
	</p>
	<p>
		Five decades on, the cast has changed. The actors are now organized crime, intelligence
		services, cyber-mercenaries. The targets are personal data, banking authentication, political
		surveillance. And on the other side of the table, activists and entire communities rebuild
		infrastructure, mesh, LoRa, dial-up, HF, satellite, to punch through state blockades.
	</p>
	<p>
		In every context, the lack of native security in legacy protocols (SS7, Diameter, SIP) and
		the ubiquity of mobile phones drive both the modernization of defenses (signaling firewalls,
		end-to-end encryption) and the sophistication of offenses. The phreaking cycle didn't close;
		it just changed octave.
	</p>

	<div class="ph-disclaimer">
		<div class="ph-disclaimer-title">⚠ DISCLAIMER</div>
		This article is strictly educational and historical. The analog techniques described haven't
		worked on modern networks for nearly 40 years. The modern techniques involve serious crimes:
		fraud, interception, unauthorized access, in every jurisdiction mentioned here. Reproducing,
		selling or operating IMSI catchers, modified femtocells or SIM boxes is a crime in Brazil
		(Lei 9.472/97, Lei 12.737/12 and successors) and in most countries. This text exists to help
		you understand the problem, not reproduce it.
	</div>
</section>
