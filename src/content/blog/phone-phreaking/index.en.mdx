<SectionDivider label="01 // ORIGINS · THE NETWORK THAT TALKED" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose">
		<h2>When the <span class="ph-accent-blue">network</span> still spoke out loud</h2>
		<p>
			Until the 1990s, the global phone system was dominated by <strong>AT&T</strong> and its
			national counterparts. It was the largest engineering project of the 20th century before
			the internet: a network of electromechanical switches (later electronic, with the ESS,
			<em>Electronic Switching Systems</em>) covering entire continents over copper wire and
			relay-based central offices.
		</p>
		<p>
			The defining feature, and the fatal flaw, was <strong class="ph-accent-orange">in-band
			signaling</strong>: the commands that controlled a call (set up, tear down, route) were
			audio tones transmitted <em>on the same frequency band as the human voice</em>. The switch
			had no separate control channel; it listened to the same wire that carried your conversation.
		</p>
		<p>
			An idle long-distance trunk continuously emitted a <strong>2600 Hz</strong> tone. When
			someone placed a call, the tone stopped, signaling "line in use". When the call ended, the
			tone returned, signaling "trunk free, next customer please". The consequence was simple and
			devastating: <em>if you could generate 2600 Hz into the receiver, the switch believed your
			call had ended, without you hanging up.</em>
		</p>
		<p>
			AT&T published all the technical details in the <strong>Bell System Technical
			Journal</strong> (November 1960, <em>"Signaling Systems for Control of Telephone
			Switching"</em>). The assumption was naive but honest: no ordinary customer would own a
			calibrated tone generator, and Bell engineers were trustworthy men. It took one generation
			to turn that trust into a billion-dollar hole.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose">
		<h2><span class="ph-accent-orange">Joybubbles</span>, the boy who talked to the network</h2>
		<p>
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joybubbles" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Josef
			Carl Engressia Jr.</strong></a>, born May 25, 1949 in Richmond, Virginia, was the first
			phreaker recorded by history. He was born blind and had <strong>absolute pitch</strong>.
		</p>

				<div class="ph-portrait-corners" aria-hidden="true">
					<span></span><span></span><span></span><span></span>

		</figure>
		<p>
			By age 4 he already spent hours on the phone. He soon grew fascinated by the thousands
			of distinct tones the network produced, and started listening to them for hours every day.
			2600 Hz wasn't a common tone on regular calls; he only began to hear it after spending
			long stretches with the phone off the hook, listening to the silence of the idle
			long-distance trunk. When he recognized that specific tone, he started whistling it back:
			he didn't yet know what it did, but he knew it was a valid system signal. He had found
			2600 Hz at age 7, in 1957, <strong>thirteen years</strong> before Captain Crunch and the
			cereal whistle. Even before the whistle, still a kid, Joe found he could replicate
			rotary-dial pulses with the hookswitch and bypass the lock his babysitter clamped on the
			phone: the first practical record of tap dialing.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-howbox">
			<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ WHAT IS ABSOLUTE PITCH?</div>
			<div>
				A rare ability (estimated at 1 in 10,000 people) to identify and reproduce any musical
				note without a reference. Someone with absolute pitch hears a sound and knows
				immediately whether it's A-440Hz, E-330Hz or G#-415Hz, the same way most people
				recognize colors. Joybubbles' gift was even rarer: he could <em>whistle</em> any
				specific frequency on demand, with just a few hertz of variation. For a phone system that
				controlled calls with calibrated tones, that was handing him the keys to the vault.
			</div>
		</div>
		<p>
			In 1968, a student at the <strong>University of South Florida</strong>, Joe was the subject
			of a campus newspaper article that nicknamed him <em>"The Whistler"</em>. AT&T sued the
			university. In 1971, Ron Rosenbaum interviewed him for the Esquire piece
			<a href="https://www.slate.com/articles/technology/the_spectator/2011/10/the_article_that_inspired_steve_jobs_secrets_of_the_little_blue_.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>"Secrets of the Little Blue Box"</em></a>,
			the same text that would inspire
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Wozniak</a>
			to build the first Blue Box.
		</p>
		<p>
			Joe never used his skills for fraud: he wanted to <em>understand the network</em>. In 1975,
			after years of friction with the FBI, he was hired by <strong>Mountain Bell</strong> in
			Denver as a network troubleshooter, a role he held for seven years until moving to
			Minneapolis in 1982. In May 1988 he "reverted to childhood" as a way to process sexual
			abuse he had suffered as a child, declaring himself permanently 5 years old. In 1991, he
			formalized the change in court and legally adopted the name
			<strong class="ph-accent-orange">"Joybubbles"</strong>. He died on August 8, 2007 in
			Minneapolis of congestive heart failure. Worth listening to NPR's obituary:
			<a href="https://www.npr.org/transcripts/13764518" target="_blank" rel="noopener">"Joe
			Engressia, Expert 'Phone Phreak,' Dies"</a> (2007).
		</p>
		<div class="ph-trivia">
			<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ JOYBUBBLES WAS NOT AN ANOMALY · THE BLIND PHREAKER COMMUNITY</div>
			Popular literature treated Joe as a genius outlier, but Phil Lapsley in
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exploding_the_Phone" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>Exploding the Phone</em></a>
			(2013) maps out an entire community: <strong>Bill Acker</strong> (New York),
			<strong>Roy Bates</strong> and <strong>Denny Teresi</strong> (California), all blind,
			all sharp-eared, all discovering the phone network as a social space at a time when
			<em>conference lines</em> and idle trunks were accessibility before the term existed.
			Anyone with free time, a phone against the ear and above-average pitch perception lived
			phreaking as routine, not feat. Joybubbles was the public face of an invisible network.
		</div>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="03 // THE TAP · HOOKSWITCH DIALING" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-grid-2 ph-mb-xl">
		<div>
			<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">tap</span>: hookswitch dialing</h2>
			<p>
				The oldest phreaking technique used no special tones or whistles, just a well-trained
				finger. Called <strong>tapping</strong> or <em>switch-hook dialing</em>, it turned
				the phone's hookswitch into an improvised dial. Joybubbles learned the trick as a
				kid, before he ever discovered the magic whistle.
			</p>
			<p>
				To understand why the trick works, it helps to see what it was imitating: the rotary
				dial telephone (alongside). Each digit spun the disk to a finger stop; on its way
				back, an internal cam rotated and its teeth struck a copper pawl, generating the
				electrical pulses the switch counted.
			</p>
		</div>
		<RotaryDial locale="en" />
	</div>

	<div class="ph-grid-2">
		<div>
			<h3>How tapping beats the dial</h3>
			<p>
				Dialing "5" meant sending 5 pulses at a cadence of <strong>10 pulses per second</strong>
				(60ms open / 40ms closed). Phreakers found the shortcut: <em>tapping the hookswitch
				repeatedly</em> opens and closes the same circuit. Same logic as the dial, no dial
				needed. Phones with a locked rotary? Bypass via hookswitch. Hotel payphones with no
				dial? Tap the hook. It was the most democratic trick in phreaking, any finger learned
				it in half an hour.
			</p>
			<div class="ph-howbox">
				<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ HOW IT WORKED</div>
				<div>
					1. Pick up the phone (line closed → dial tone)<br/>
					2. To dial "3", tap the hookswitch 3 times (~60ms each)<br/>
					3. Wait ~700ms, the switch registers "3"<br/>
					4. Repeat for the other digits. "0" is 10 taps.
				</div>
			</div>
			<h3>Cadence: 40/60 (Bell) vs 33/67 (CCITT/Telebrás)</h3>
			<p>
				The numbers above describe the <strong>Bell US</strong> cadence: each pulse is
				<span class="ph-mono">60 ms break / 40 ms make</span> (60% circuit open, 40%
				closed), at 10 PPS. Under the <strong>CCITT</strong> standard that Telebrás (the
				Brazilian state telecom monopoly) inherited in 1968, the ratio is different:
				<span class="ph-mono">67 ms break / 33 ms make</span>. Same frequency (10 pulses
				per second), different internal distribution.
			</p>
			<p>
				In practice, a trained finger could fool any switch, because relays had wide
				tolerance for deviation. But the difference explains why imported blue boxes and
				dialers had to be recalibrated for Brazil, and why BR phreakers ended up designing
				their own circuits instead of cloning American schematics without adjustment.
			</p>
			<div class="ph-trivia">
				<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ POP CULTURE</div>
				The classic scene of a prisoner who needs to make a forbidden call, hangs up the
				prison payphone and <em>taps the hookswitch</em> to dial a number off the approved
				list, is a direct reference to this technique. It shows up in
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hackers_(film)" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>Hackers (1995)</strong></a>,
				in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WarGames" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>WarGames (1983)</em></a>,
				and in a dozen prison thrillers from the 80s and 90s. It wasn't a screenwriter's
				invention: it was the only thing that actually worked on a phone with a locked dial.
			</div>
		</div>
		<div>
			<figure class="ph-figure ph-mb-sm">
				<Image src={lockedPhone} alt="Rotary phone with a physical padlock through the dial" widths={[400, 800]} sizes="(max-width: 768px) 90vw, 420px" />

			</figure>

<SectionDivider label="04 // THE 2600 Hz WHISTLE" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-grid-2">
		<div>
			<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">2600 Hz</span> whistle</h2>
			<p>
				Joybubbles was the first to notice, but not the only one. In 1968, engineer
				<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Draper" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong class="ph-accent-orange">John Draper</strong></a>
				met a blind sailor who gave him the tip: the plastic toy whistle that came as a prize
				in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cap%27n_Crunch" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Cap'n Crunch</a>
				cereal played exactly 2600 Hz. Blow it into the mouthpiece and the long-distance trunk
				surrendered. The world got a <em>Captain Crunch</em>; AT&T lost control.
			</p>
			<WhistleViewer locale="en" />
			<p>
				When the whistle hit the receiver, the switch believed the call had ended, but the line
				stayed open. You were still connected to a live long-distance trunk, only now <em>as if
				you were the switch yourself</em>, free to issue direct commands.
			</p>
			<div class="ph-howbox">
				<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ HOW IT WORKED</div>
				<div>
					1. Dial a 0800 number (free, but uses a long-distance trunk)<br/>
					2. After connection, blow 2600 Hz into the mouthpiece<br/>
					3. The remote office disconnects the destination but keeps the trunk<br/>
					4. You now have direct access to the trunk network
				</div>
			</div>
		</div>

	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="05 // BLUE BOX & CCITT5" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-xl">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-blue">Blue Box</span> and MF tones</h2>
		<p>
			Once the 2600 Hz whistle "opened" the trunk, you had to <em>tell the network</em> where to
			dial. AT&T used a signaling system called <strong class="ph-accent-blue">CCITT5 / Multi-Frequency (MF)</strong>:
			each digit is represented by <strong>two simultaneous tones</strong>, picked from a matrix of
			6 frequencies (700, 900, 1100, 1300, 1500, 1700 Hz).
		</p>
		<p>
			The <strong class="ph-accent-blue">Blue Box</strong>, popularized by
			<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Wozniak" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Wozniak</a>
			and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Jobs" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Steve Jobs</a>
			before they founded Apple, was a device that generated those tone pairs. Ron Rosenbaum's
			article <a href="https://www.slate.com/articles/technology/the_spectator/2011/10/the_article_that_inspired_steve_jobs_secrets_of_the_little_blue_.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><em>"Secrets of the Little Blue Box"</em></a>,
			published in Esquire in 1971, exploded the popularity of the hobby.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-howbox">
			<div class="ph-howbox-title">▸ BEFORE YOU PLAY: KP AND ST</div>
			<div>
				On top of the 10 digits, the MF protocol carries two <em>control</em> tones that tell
				the remote switch when an address starts and ends. Without them, the digit sequence is
				just noise.<br/><br/>
				• <strong class="ph-accent-blue">KP</strong> (<em>Key Pulse</em>, tones
				<span class="ph-mono">1100 + 1700 Hz</span>) opens the packet: it says
				"<em>heads up, digits are coming</em>". Always the first tone sent.<br/>
				• <strong class="ph-accent-blue">ST</strong> (<em>Start</em>, tones
				<span class="ph-mono">1500 + 1700 Hz</span>) closes the packet: "<em>I'm done, you
				can route now</em>". Always the last.<br/><br/>
				The full sequence for a call is <span class="ph-mono">KP · digits · ST</span>. In the
				simulator below, start with KP, dial the destination digits, and finish with ST.
			</div>
		</div>
	</div>
	<BlueBox locale="en" />

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mt-lg">
		<h3>But Brazil was different: R2/MFC 5C</h3>
		<p>
			While AT&T, BT and European carriers ran CCITT5 with in-band MF tones, in 1968 Brazil
			adopted the <strong class="ph-accent-orange">R2/MFC variant 5C</strong> signaling.
			The difference is architectural, not cosmetic: in R2, MF tones move between
			<em>registers</em> (sender/receiver only allocated during setup), never on the voice
			channel. Supervision runs at <span class="ph-mono">3825 Hz out-of-band</span>, above the
			PCM codec passband, filtered out before it ever reaches the mouthpiece.
		</p>
		<p>
			Playing <span class="ph-mono">1380 + 1500 Hz</span> into the microphone was just noise.
			The blue box that seized trunks in New York and London died silent in Brazil.
		</p>
		<p>
			The Brazilian blue box only worked over <strong>international 0800 routes</strong> that
			terminated on still-live C5 trunks: Mexico, Costa Rica, Guatemala, Hong Kong. AT&T
			USADirect (<span class="ph-mono">0800-890-0288</span>), MCI WorldPhone
			(<span class="ph-mono">0800-890-0012</span>) and Sprint Express
			(<span class="ph-mono">0800-888-8000</span>) were literally the doors BR phreakers used
			to cross the ocean.
		</p>
		<p>
			The mechanism is the third scenario in the diagram below: on the BR leg, the Blue Box's
			MF tones travel as plain voice and cause no harm: R2 keeps signaling separate. But the
			0800 gateway repackages the call onto an international C5 trunk, where voice and commands
			share the same channel again. Out there, the same tones become commands and seize the
			trunk. The victim is the foreign carrier, crossed via Telebrás without a billing record.
			For everything else, R2 was architectural defense. Technical intro to R2:
			<a href="http://soft-switch.org/unicall/mfcr2/ch02.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">soft-switch.org/unicall/mfcr2/ch02</a>.
		</p>
	</div>

		<h3>Boxes that never wore <span class="ph-accent-orange">blue or red</span></h3>
		<p>
			The international color-box catalog (blue, red, black, green, etc.) lists no Brazilian
			variants. When the network changed architecture, the tricks changed color, but only some
			of them ever got documented in foreign zines. Three BR variants belong on the table next
			to the others:
		</p>
		<ul class="ph-list">
			<li><strong>1N4007 diode</strong> (the "<em>Brazilian black box</em>"): diode + 22 kΩ
				resistor wired into a token-payphone (TUP) line. Blocked the polarity reversal that
				signaled "call answered". The switch kept thinking the call was still ringing.
				Worked on electromechanical switches until billing migrated to 12 kHz metering pulses
				in the 90s.</li>
			<li><strong>Pencil-traced / nail-polished card</strong> (Generation 1, 1992-95): the
				Brazilian inductive phone card attack, detailed in interactive form in the next section.
				Graphite reconnects burned tracks; clear nail polish keeps the next read from blowing
				new cells.</li>
			<li><strong>The flour trick</strong>: a wheat-flour paste applied to a token slot. The
				paste hardened and gummed up the solenoid of the magnetic see-saw mechanism, locking it
				in the "token accepted" position. Infinite credit until someone smashed the booth. More
				destructive than effective, but part of the neighborhood repertoire.</li>
		</ul>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mt-lg">
		<h3>BADISCO · the Brazilian <span class="ph-accent-orange">lineman handset</span></h3>
		<p>
			The BR version of the Beige Box has its own name: <strong>badisco</strong>. Not a
			homemade hack. It's the commercial operator's phone sold to Telebrás technicians, then
			Telefônica/Embratel, today Claro/Vivo/Oi. <em>Multitoc MU256T</em>, <em>Intelbras
			TC 20</em>, <em>Solução</em>: compact units with alligator clips ("crocodile") instead of
			the American <em>bed-of-nails</em>. Connects directly to the twisted pair inside the
			street's Krone M10 cabinet or the CTA box on the building facade.
		</p>
		<p>
			Sales were never restricted: <strong>Santa Ifigênia</strong> and <strong>Galeria
			Pajé</strong> in São Paulo, <strong>Saara</strong> in Rio. For the teenage BR phreaker,
			R$ 30 on a generic badisco or a repurposed dollar-store headset (with 4 wires, of which
			only two matter) unlocked the entire analog network of the neighborhood. Under every
			sidewalk, an unencrypted copper pair. Listening was a question of motivation.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="07 // PHONE CARD · A BRAZILIAN INVENTION" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">phone card</span>: Brazil's contribution to phreaking</h2>
		<p>
			While Americans hid <em>blue boxes</em> in backpacks, Brazil was writing its own chapter of
			telephony history with 100% domestic technology. In July 1976, engineer
			<strong>Nelson Guilherme Bardini</strong> (B.S. Civil Engineering 1962, Electronics 1963 from
			Mackenzie University) started developing inside TELESP what he called the
			<em>"Electronic Token"</em>: a PVC card with coils and microfuses that replaced the round
			metal token used in payphones.
		</p>
		<p>
			The project won the Telebras research-category award and earned the
			<strong>Landell de Moura Prize</strong>. The invention spread worldwide, but only became
			commercial in Brazil itself in <strong>1992</strong>: the first card was issued at the
			Brazilian Formula 1 Grand Prix in Interlagos, and the official rollout came with
			<strong class="ph-accent-blue">Eco-92</strong>, in Rio. The motivation was practical: metal
			tokens suffered constant vandalism, and the cost of collecting them from payphones broke the
			whole model.
		</p>

		<div class="ph-trivia">
			<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ PATENT PI 7804885 AND THE PASTA MACHINE</div>
			Bardini filed patent <strong>PI 7804885</strong> on <strong>1978-07-28</strong>; the
			grant came through at the INPI on <strong>1984-03-27</strong>, almost six years later,
			long enough for the Brazilian inductive card to debut in 1992 as the first commercial
			implementation of this technology anywhere in the world. The domestic detail that
			survives in oral history: the first prototypes of the tin-lead (Sn-Pb) alloy used for
			the tracks were pressed at home, using a <em>homemade pasta machine</em> adapted as a
			cold press. The card that would become a collector's item, a generational badge and a
			phreaking vector was born in a kitchen, before it became industrial engineering. Every
			invention needs a kitchen.
		</div>

		<h3>How the inductive card worked</h3>
		<p>
			The principle is <strong>Faraday's law of induction</strong> (1831): an alternating current in
			one coil generates a magnetic field; a second nearby coil captures that field and returns a
			proportional voltage. On the back of the card, several inductive <em>cells</em>, small metal
			tracks in series with microfuses, were read by the terminal:
		</p>
		<ul class="ph-list">
			<li><strong>Read</strong>: a low current passes through the coil; the terminal measures the impedance and knows whether the cell is open or closed.</li>
			<li><strong>Charge</strong>: when a unit is consumed, the terminal injects enough current to <em>blow</em> the microfuse for that cell. Open = unit spent.</li>
			<li><strong>Credit</strong>: the number of intact cells is the balance. When all are blown, the card is discarded.</li>
		</ul>

		<h3>The graphite bypass</h3>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-bypass-layout">
		<div>
			<p>
				The inductive card was robust against the obvious attack (cloning a metal token was trivial),
				but it had a physical weakness: the circuit was <em>visible</em> on the back. Starting in the
				late 90s, BBSs and mailing lists circulated a trick that became folklore: running a
				<strong>graphite pencil</strong> over the burned tracks reconnected the circuit. Graphite is
				electrically conductive (resistivity around 10⁻⁵ Ω·m), and a dense enough layer brought the
				cell back to a state the terminal would read as intact. Variants applied
				<em>clear nail polish</em> over the area to prevent the next read from blowing fresh fuses,
				freezing the balance.
			</p>
			<p>
				The demo on the side is a didactic version: click <strong>INSERT CARD</strong> and watch the
				tracks burn one by one as the credit drops to zero. Then,
				<strong class="ph-accent-orange">drag the cursor over the card</strong> as if you were
				penciling: the tracks reconnect, the balance returns. Later card generations mitigated the
				problem with cryptographic cells and opaque back coatings, but for a long time, the graphite
				trick was the most Brazilian way to phreak.
			</p>
		</div>

		<PhoneCardBypass locale="en" />
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mt-lg">
		<h3>The telecartofilia culture</h3>
		<p>
			Low cost, nationwide distribution and visual variety turned the card into a
			<strong>cultural phenomenon</strong>. From 1994 onward, commemorative series, F1, World Cup,
			dinosaurs, cartoon characters, landscapes, became collector's items. A new hobby emerged,
			<strong>telecartofilia</strong>, sister to philately and numismatics, with clubs, catalogs,
			swap fairs and limited runs commanding small fortunes. Companies, schools and government
			agencies commissioned their own designs; manufacturing defects became prized rarities. The
			hobby lasted until around 2010, when prepaid mobile phones retired the payphone and, with
			it, the card itself.
		</p>
	</div>

</section>

<SectionDivider label="08 // BRAZIL · THE SCENE THAT WHISTLED IN PORTUGUESE" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-md">
		<h2>The scene that whistled in <span class="ph-accent-orange">Portuguese</span></h2>
		<p>
			Between 1985 and 2005, Brazil had its own phreaker scene. Not a colony of the American
			one: R2/MFC blocked the classic blue box, per-pulse billing imposed a chronotype, and
			urban infrastructure (payphones on every corner, Krone cabinets open on the sidewalk,
			twisted pairs exposed) opened vectors that didn't exist in Manhattan or Berkeley. The
			international literature skips this chapter. The living sources are fifty years old now
			and sit in front of the same computer, just with a different job.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<h3>1998 to 1999 · The <span class="ph-accent-orange">170-meter</span> wiretap</h3>
		<p>
			São Paulo periphery, 1998. Four payphones on a single corner. One of them, picked for
			distance to the home of a 12-year-old named Gutem, got a little plastic sign:
			<em>"out of order"</em>. Behind the sign, a 170-meter twisted pair running pole to
			backyard, connecting the payphone to an AMD K6-2 400 MHz and a 28.8 kbps modem. The
			<em>internet wiretap</em> was the cheapest possible infrastructure for pulling research
			files at 3 kB/s into the small hours.
		</p>
		<div class="ph-trivia">
			<div class="ph-trivia-title">▸ TESTIMONY · THE NOTE FOR THE TECHNICIAN</div>
			<blockquote class="ph-quote ph-quote--accent ph-quote--block">
				"I left some stuff written on the back of the paper specifically for the Telesp
				operator, you know?
				Listen man, this is the poor kid who tapped the wire just to stay on the
				internet. Straight up, do whatever you want. There must have been some guys who
				left it there because it ran for a good while. But that was a few months, you
				know? Until I worked out a schedule to get on late at night. I'd already done
				some serious research on where to find material."
			</blockquote>
		</div>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<h3>The tariff that turned <span class="ph-accent-orange">a generation</span> nocturnal</h3>
		<p>
			Telesp charged <strong>1 single pulse</strong> between <span class="ph-mono">00:00 and
			06:00</span> on weekdays, and from <span class="ph-mono">Saturday 14:00 through Monday
			06:00</span>. Outside that window, the bill came multiplied by 8. The first afternoon
			usage bill arrived at <strong>R$ 320</strong> (in 1998 the minimum wage was R$ 130).
			The result: an entire generation of BR phreakers and nerds inverted their routine:
			research between 23:00 and 01:00, downloads queued in the manager, sleep between 06:00
			and 14:00. The BR phreaker chronotype was imposed by the tariff. Not a preference,
			household accounting.
		</p>
		<TarifaClock locale="en" />
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<h3>The zines and <span class="ph-accent-orange">#phreak</span> on BrasNet</h3>
		<p>
			The scene barely documented itself, and only among locals. <strong>Barata Elétrica</strong>,
			started in 1994 by <strong>Derneval "Curupira" Cunha</strong>, was the first
			Portuguese-language hacker e-zine. Issue 7 (<em>"Os Maníacos por Telefone"</em>)
			translated and adapted Bruce Sterling's Bell System chapters into the Telebrás context.
			In parallel, on the <span class="ph-mono">#phreak</span> channel of the
			<strong>BrasNet</strong> network, Phroide, Dialtone, Psylon and Papillon kept the group
			<strong>PhreaKhaos</strong> alive. Phroide's line was the manifesto:
			<em>"Fuck all americans phreakers"</em>, not as an insult, but as a claim: our phreaking
			is not a copy. Other zines from the era: <strong>Axur 05</strong>,
			<strong>Unsekurity Magazine</strong>, <strong>Pr0j3kt M4yh3m</strong>. Surviving mirrors:
			<a href="https://absoluta.org/barata" target="_blank" rel="noopener">absoluta.org/barata</a>
			and <a href="https://sites.google.com/site/barataeletricafanzine" target="_blank" rel="noopener">sites.google.com/site/barataeletricafanzine</a>.
			The favorite phreaker software (<a href="https://github.com/Jimvin/BlueBEEP" target="_blank" rel="noopener">BlueBEEP</a>,
			archived at <a href="https://archive.org/details/bbeep-006" target="_blank" rel="noopener">archive.org/details/bbeep-006</a>)
			generated MF C5 tones from a PC with a sound card, no analog hack needed.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose">
		<h3>The end of Brazilian <span class="ph-accent-orange">analog phreaking</span> · 2006–2010</h3>
		<p>
			ADSL broadband reached Gutem's neighborhood in 2006. The migration to prepaid mobile was
			already underway, and the payphone began to disappear from the landscape. Around 2010,
			analog BR phreaking had ended for the generation that lived it. Not by repression, by
			network obsolescence. As he sums it up: <em>"The alternatives were to switch to mobile
			and I didn't have the money for that yet."</em> When he came back to the hacker scene,
			it was already 2010, already IP, already something else. The badisco became a museum piece
			before turning back into a R$ 1 store item.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="09 // MODERN ATTACKS · 2018–2025" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-sm">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">ghosts</span> changed instruments</h2>
		<p>
			Digital telephony killed analog phreaking, but traded one set of fragile protocols for
			another. <Glossary def="Signaling System #7. The language carriers use to swap commands between themselves: 'give me this number's location', 'this SMS goes to that subscriber'. Designed in 1975 for a closed club of trusted operators, no authentication or encryption.">SS7</Glossary>, <Glossary def="IP successor to SS7, the 4G/LTE standard. Inherited the same original sins: weak inter-carrier validation, critical messages without strong authentication.">Diameter</Glossary>, <Glossary def="GPRS Tunnelling Protocol. Carries data traffic between mobile network nodes. When exposed to the internet without a firewall, it becomes an open door for roaming attacks.">GTP</Glossary>, <Glossary def="Signaling Transport: SS7 over IP. Reduces operational cost but widens the attack surface: any server with a rented SCCP address can inject messages.">SIGTRAN</Glossary>, <Glossary def="IP Multimedia Subsystem. A full SIP stack inside the carrier, the foundation of VoLTE. In 2021 researchers demonstrated downgrade and interception attacks on IMS calls.">IMS</Glossary>: stacks designed
			in the 1970s–1990s, when the network was a closed club of trusted carriers. Today, any
			actor with access to a <em>roaming hub</em> or a <Glossary def="Software-Defined Radio. A radio whose modulation lives entirely in software. A US$ 300 HackRF or a US$ 700 USRP transmits in any mobile telephony band; what used to be carrier-grade hardware costs the price of a mid-tier phone.">SDR</Glossary> of US$ 500 enters as an authorized peer.
		</p>
	</div>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">09.1</span> SS7 / SIGTRAN</h3>
	<p class="ph-prose">
		The <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signalling_System_No._7" target="_blank" rel="noopener">SS7 protocol</a>
		(1975) and its IP version, SIGTRAN, are the baseline signaling for 2G/3G worldwide.
		<strong>No authentication</strong>, no encryption. Anyone inside the network can query a
		subscriber's location, redirect SMS, or intercept calls. In 2019, fraudsters
		<a href="https://www.vice.com/en/article/criminals-hackers-ss7-uk-banks-metro-bank/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">exploited SS7 in the UK</a>
		to intercept 2FA codes and drain <strong>Metro Bank</strong> accounts. In Brazil, similar
		fraud was already documented since 2016. The
		<a href="https://www.enisa.europa.eu/publications/signalling-security-in-telecom-ss7-diameter-5g" target="_blank" rel="noopener">2018 ENISA report</a>
		formally acknowledges the technical debt: 2G/3G rely on SS7 with no "modern security
		considerations", and 4G (Diameter) inherited the problem.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">09.2</span> Femtocells and rogue base stations</h3>
	<p class="ph-prose">
		In 2013, <strong>Doug DePerry</strong> and <strong>Tom Ritter</strong> showed at
		<a href="https://www.defcon.org/images/defcon-21/dc-21-presentations/DePerry-Ritter/DEFCON-21-DePerry-Ritter-Femtocell-Updated.pdf" target="_blank" rel="noopener">DEF CON 21</a>
		how to modify a Verizon <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femtocell" target="_blank" rel="noopener">femtocell</a>
		(US$ 250) plus a Raspberry Pi (US$ 50) to listen to every call and SMS from nearby phones.
		The whole rig fit in a backpack. Twelve years later, the same technique came back at scale:
		commercial gear like <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stingray_phone_tracker" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>StingRay</strong></a>
		(Harris Corp.) and homemade variants with <strong><Glossary def="Universal Software Radio Peripheral. High-end SDR (US$ 700–4,000) used in telecom research and sophisticated attacks against mobile networks.">USRP</Glossary> +
		<a href="https://openbts.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">OpenBTS</a> /
		<a href="https://osmocom.org/" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Osmocom</a></strong> are used
		by states, gangs and researchers. Phones connect to the strongest signal, and when the
		strongest signal is hostile, hostile wins.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">09.3</span> SIM Swap and number portability</h3>
	<p class="ph-prose">
		A telco support agent talked into transferring a victim's line to an attacker-controlled SIM.
		Within seconds, every 2FA SMS, bank, email, exchange, goes to the wrong phone.
		<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIM_swap_scam" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>SIM swap</strong></a>
		doesn't exploit a protocol: it exploits people. But the metrics climb with SMS-based 2FA. The
		FBI and Brazil's ANATEL issued public warnings in the early 2020s.
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">09.4</span> VoLTE / IMS / SMS Blasting</h3>
	<p class="ph-prose">
		Voice over LTE (<Glossary def="Voice over LTE. Instead of analog circuit-switched voice, calls become IP packets over the 4G network. Better quality, larger attack surface: signaling becomes SIP.">VoLTE</Glossary>) brought IMS, a full SIP stack inside the carrier. Researchers
		demonstrated downgrade and interception attacks on VoLTE calls in 2021. In parallel,
		<strong><Glossary def="Hardware rack with dozens to hundreds of SIM chips running in parallel. Originally used to dodge international tariffs; today it's the infrastructure behind industrialized SMS phishing scams.">SIM Boxes</Glossary></strong>, racks with hundreds of SIM cards running in parallel, fire tens
		of thousands of SMS per hour for financial fraud. Same idea as the Blue Box, industrialized
		(see Section 14).
	</p>

	<h3><span class="ph-tag">09.5</span> PBX and VoIP fraud</h3>
	<p class="ph-prose">
		Misconfigured corporate <Glossary def="Private Branch Exchange. The corporate phone switch. When poorly configured, it becomes an open door for toll fraud: the attacker dials premium international numbers using the company's line (and its bill).">PBX</Glossary>s keep dialing premium-rate international numbers overnight.
		Hacked <Glossary def="Analog Telephone Adapter. The little box that connects a regular analog phone to a VoIP line. When hacked, it can route calls through the attacker without the owner noticing.">ATA</Glossary> adapters, weak SIP credentials, exposed VoIP gateways, each one is a modern
		<em>blue box</em>, generating five-figure invoices for the victim and dividends for the
		attacker.
	</p>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="12 // OPERATORS · THE WOMEN THE SCENE FORGOT" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-md">
		<h2>The <span class="ph-accent-orange">women</span> the scene forgot</h2>
		<p>
			AT&T started out hiring <em>boys</em> as telephone operators in 1878. Within a few years
			it abandoned the practice. The explanation is in Bruce Sterling, in <em>The Hacker
			Crackdown</em> (1992), quoted by Derneval in issue 7 of Barata Elétrica:
		</p>
		<blockquote class="ph-quote">
			"It was the boys who were hired first as operators. Then it turned out women made less
			noise, were more discreet, and less tempted to prank-call with the phones."
		</blockquote>
		<p>
			The boys did <em>phreaking</em> before the word existed: prank calls, redirects for fun,
			long conversations with customers. Women were preferred because they <em>didn't</em> do
			that. Forty years later, the pendulum reversed: some of the best phreakers in history
			were exactly the women who knew the network from the inside, because they worked in it
			or lived alongside someone who did. Popular literature didn't record them; two who
			survived in fragments are below.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<h3>Susan "Susy Thunder" <span class="ph-accent-orange">Headley</span></h3>
		<p>
			Los Angeles, late 70s and 80s. Susan Headley did <em>pretexting</em> against Pacific Bell
			operators before <em>social engineering</em> entered the hacker vocabulary. She called
			central offices posing as internal staff, requested routes and codes, got them.
			<em>Garbology</em>: she went through dumpsters behind PacBell buildings looking for
			discarded technical manuals. ABC's <em>20/20</em> program interviewed Geraldo Rivera about
			the scene in 1982; Headley appears in the footage. Her DEF CON 3 keynote (1995) coined a
			line that could be the epigraph of the rest of this article:
		</p>
		<blockquote class="ph-quote ph-quote--fg">
			"Being a human being is a vulnerability by itself."
		</blockquote>
		<p>
			The full profile in <em>The Verge</em> (2022,
			<a href="https://www.theverge.com/c/22889425/susy-thunder-headley-hackers-phone-phreaks-claire-evans" target="_blank" rel="noopener">"Searching for Susy Thunder"</a>,
			by Claire Evans) is today the best primary source on her. Recommended read.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<h3>Chanda <span class="ph-accent-orange">Leir</span></h3>
		<p>
			January 1989. <em>Phrack #24</em> publishes her <em>Pro-Phile</em>, conducted by
			<a href="http://www.phrack.org/issues/24/2.html" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Taran King</a>,
			with an intro that says more about the scene than about the interviewee:
		</p>
		<blockquote class="ph-quote">
			"one of the more rare sights in the world of phreaking and hacking, a female!"
		</blockquote>
		<p>
			Chanda was the only woman with a Pro-Phile in Phrack until issue 32. She conferenced over
			<em>loops</em>, knew MF tones, operated on the network with gender-neutral nicks because
			the safest way to be respected as a phreaker was not to be identified as a woman.
			Systemic (not aesthetic) pseudonymity erases gender from the historical record. It's hard
			to count a community that documented itself under nicks. Rather than "there were few
			women", the honest version is: <em>we don't know how many there were, because the culture
			didn't let them identify themselves</em>.
		</p>
	</div>

	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-lg">
		<p class="ph-prose-note">
			The documentation of women's participation in phreaking is structurally short because the
			culture was structurally hostile. Systemic pseudonymity hid gender. Conferences were
			<em>boys' clubs</em>. And when a woman did publish, the byline came with an asterisk
			(<em>(female)</em>, <em>a female!</em>, <em>rare sights</em>), signaling that this was
			notable precisely because it was exceptional. The documentary invisibility is, in
			itself, the finding.
		</p>
	</div>
</section>

<SectionDivider label="13 // RESISTANCE UNDER AUTHORITARIAN REGIMES" />

<section class="ph-section">
	<div class="ph-prose ph-mb-md">
		<h2>When the state <span class="ph-accent-orange">switches off</span> the network</h2>
		<p>
			When governments cut mobile or fixed internet to silence protests, communities improvise.
			The result is an unlikely catalog: amateur radio, V.92 modems crossing a border, mesh
			networks, LoRaWAN, satellite. The official network falls; others emerge.
		</p>
	</div>

<SectionDivider label="19 // LEGACY AND FINAL THOUGHTS" />

<section class="ph-outro">
	<p>
		In the 1980s, AT&T began migrating to the <strong>CCS7
		(Common Channel Signaling)</strong> system, which separates signaling from the audio channel. From
		that point on, no audible tone could control the network anymore, analog phreaking was dead.
	</p>
	<p>
		But its legacy is immense. <strong>Wozniak</strong> and <strong>Jobs</strong> sold blue boxes in
		UC Berkeley dorms before founding Apple. <strong>Joybubbles</strong> became folklore. The magazine
		<em>2600: The Hacker Quarterly</em>, founded in 1984, takes its name from the magical tone. And the
		ethos, radical curiosity, reverse engineering closed systems, became the DNA of hacker culture.
	</p>
	<p>
		Five decades on, the cast has changed. The actors are now organized crime, intelligence
		services, cyber-mercenaries. The targets are personal data, banking authentication, political
		surveillance. And on the other side of the table, activists and entire communities rebuild
		infrastructure, mesh, LoRa, dial-up, HF, satellite, to punch through state blockades.
	</p>
	<p>
		In every context, the lack of native security in legacy protocols (SS7, Diameter, SIP) and
		the ubiquity of mobile phones drive both the modernization of defenses (signaling firewalls,
		end-to-end encryption) and the sophistication of offenses. The phreaking cycle didn't close;
		it just changed octave.
	</p>
